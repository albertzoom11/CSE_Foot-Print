Foot-Print

Foot-Print looks to help prevent long-term foot injuries.






Our Inspiration


We were inspired to create a product like Foot-Print because of the benefits that wearable sensors can provide in terms of prevention. The video shown above provides a thorough comparison of the benefits of prevention vs. treatment. We wanted to be able to create a product that was capable of helping people stray from injury through safe practices. Running and walking are so common in everyday life that the long-term medical effects can accumulate through bad form. Foot-print looks to detect that bad form early on by looking at the forces that each part of the foot absorbs during a run.

There are several designs that influenced both our decision in the problem statement and from which we decided to improve. While there aren’t many products that focus on collecting data on running form, the ones that do, advertise large improvements. The design that we found that seemed to meet our problem statement best was the Lumo Run Sensor, which is a product design for placement on the shoulder and tracks cadence, bounce, pelvic movement and how much brake applied with each step. It sends this data to the Lumo app, along with personalized recommendations for pre- and post-run exercises based on performance. While it doesn’t actually track the force put into each step (it can’t as it isn’t attached to the foot), it does create an interface between the product and an app to track progress and provide suggestions. We decided this feature would be important in creating our design.

There is a running shoe that has sensors in its midsole that reads as one runs and provides auditory feedback for what to improve as one runs. It is called Altra Torin G, and it does not have an app to coordinate with the sensors. Furthermore, it only has 1-2 force sensors to detect overall force and the detail is very few and far between.

We took both of these designs and combined them to narrow our design to gain more detail to where weight is shifting in the foot and to provide suggestions based on that using an app. We also decided from the beginning that providing something like a heatmap would be beneficial to providing a visual representation of the data. Each of the ideas below were designed around these specifications we decided on.



Hardware
A NodeMCU ESP8266 WiFi Module is used to provide fast live updating Firebase data through FlexiForce sensors in the Foot-Print insoles. The Arduino module calculates the average force through the duration of the run and stores it to be authenticated.
Website Interface
A user-friendly website interface for live tracking of data as well as firebase authentication to insure the security and privacy of your information. Simplistic but elegant heat maps are displayed with user feedback and a customizable journal for a visual and interactive data viewing experience.
